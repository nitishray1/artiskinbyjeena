Skincare Routine 3 min read

Why your skincare products might not be working — and it's probably not the products

You have spent real money. You have been consistent. Your skin still isn't where you want it to be. Here is the honest reason why.

You've bought the serums. You've followed the routines. You've tried the thing that worked for your friend, and the thing the dermatologist on TikTok swears by. You've been reasonably consistent. And your skin still isn't where you want it to be.

The frustrating truth is that most skincare routines fail not because of the products in them, but because of the four things that happen before the products are even opened. Let's go through them.

1. The products aren't matched to your actual skin type

This is the most common problem — and the most invisible. Most people don't actually know their skin type. They think they do, based on how their skin feels, what they've been told, or what they've assumed over years. But skin type isn't constant, and it's also easy to misread.

Oily skin is often dehydrated skin in disguise. The skin produces excess oil as a response to being stripped of moisture — often by cleansers that are too harsh, or environments that are too dry. A person who thinks they need mattifying, oil-controlling products may actually need hydration. And when they add more oil-stripping products to an already-dehydrated barrier, their skin fights back — producing more oil.

Dry skin and dehydrated skin are not the same thing. Dry skin is a skin type (low sebum production). Dehydrated skin is a condition (low water content) that can affect any skin type, including oily skin. The products that fix one don't necessarily fix the other.

Using the wrong products for the wrong skin type doesn't just mean you don't see results — it can actively make your skin worse, disrupt your barrier function, and create new problems that the right products would have prevented.

2. Your skin was never properly assessed before you started

Most skincare routines are built from the outside in — you see a product, you try it, you build a routine around things that seem to work or that you can afford. What almost never happens first is a proper assessment of what your skin actually looks like beneath the surface.

Without that foundation — without knowing whether your skin is dehydrated, congested, sun-damaged, or sensitised — any routine is essentially a guess. Sometimes a reasonable guess. But a guess.

This is exactly what a professional skin assessment addresses. Before recommending a single product or treatment, Jeena uses UV imaging to see what's actually happening in your skin — the conditions you can't see in a mirror, the patterns that only appear under professional light analysis. That's not a sales tool. It's the only way to build a routine that has any guarantee of working for your specific skin.

Skincare without a proper skin assessment is like taking medication without a diagnosis. You might get lucky. Or you might make things worse.

3. The application order is wrong

Skincare products work in a specific sequence based on molecular weight and pH. Active ingredients — vitamin C, retinol, AHAs, niacinamide — need to be applied in a particular order to penetrate the skin effectively, and in some combinations, should not be applied at the same time at all.

The general rule is thinnest to thickest (water-based serums before oils, actives before moisturiser), but the specifics matter. Applying a heavier product before a lighter one creates a barrier that prevents the lighter product from absorbing. Layering incompatible actives can cause irritation, reduce efficacy, or — in some cases — create new skin problems.

A well-built personalised routine has a specific order for a reason. It's not arbitrary. Understanding why things go where they go makes the difference between a routine that works and one that sits on top of your skin without doing much.

4. Consistency is being underestimated

This is the quietest killer of every skincare routine. Not a dramatic product failure — just inconsistency that compounds over weeks and months into no results.

Most active ingredients take 8–12 weeks of consistent use to show meaningful results. In a world where everything is instant, that timeline is easy to abandon. A product gets replaced after two weeks because it "doesn't seem to be working." A new trend appears. The routine shifts. And the skin never gets enough sustained contact with anything to actually change.

This is partly why Jeena's approach includes follow-up and accountability — not as an upsell, but because without it, most people quietly drift away from their routine and then wonder why nothing changed. Checking in at 4 weeks, adjusting what isn't working, and staying consistent with what is: that's where results come from.

What actually fixes it

A proper skin consultation — one that starts with understanding your skin before recommending anything — solves most of this immediately. You find out your actual skin type. You see the conditions present beneath the surface. You get a routine built around what your skin genuinely needs, in the right order, with realistic expectations about timeline.

That's it. No magic product. No expensive new technology. Just starting from the right place.
